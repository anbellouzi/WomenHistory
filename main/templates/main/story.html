Story of Radium Girls

Women as Dailpainters

During WWI when American men were off to war, American women went to the factories to replace the displaced men. Some women found employment as dailpainters enticing. The work entails that the women paint radioluminescent numerals on watch faces. The job was attractive because they could be inside, they could wear long skirts and shirtwaists, they did delicate work, and the pay was relatively good. The wristwatch industry was booming because soldiers on the front needed them to tell time and to be on time to fight, eat, sleep, or stay awake.

Discovery of Luminous Paint

To make the watch more enticing and functional the companies came up with a formula that made the numbers on the watch face glow in the dark by adding in a minuscule amount of radium to their paint mixture.
More on Radium

Lip...Drip...Paint

In order to get the tip of the paint brushes as thin as possible to paint the sharpest numbers on the watches, they were instructed to put the brush in their mouths. During this time in history radium was considered a cure-all and no one mind working with radium.

Emergence of Strange Diseases

In the 1920s and 1930s some dailpainters and former dailpainters began to suffer from a variety of illnesses, sometimes leading to deaths. The most prominent illness was infection in the jaw, it was called jaw rot or jaw necrosis. At first everyone thought it was caused by phosphorus and they labeled the illness as ‘phosy jaw’, which frequently happened in fireworks factories because they were working with phosphorus.

Radium Instead of Phosphorus

Eventually, a chemical analysis was done on the paint used by the dailpainters had no phorphorous in it, and the author concluded it was the radium that was causing the jaw rotting infections in the dailpainters. The labor department took no action and dailworkers were starting to die. Radium companies continued to deny that the deaths of the dailpainters were their fault while implementing strategies to contain the threat that radium poisoning posed to their businesses.

Women vs U.S. Radium Company

On May 18, 1927 five women banded together to file a lawsuit against the US Radium Corporation and they were later dubbed as the Radium Girls. They were Grace Fryer, Edna Hussman, Katherine Schaub, and sisters Quinta McDonald and Albina Larice. Met with lots of resistance because scientists refused to testify for their case. It wasn’t until more cases of deaths from radium made it to the newspaper and the women sought help from the Consumers’ League that radium companies started to cave and settled with suits brought to them. Consumers’ League was an organization working for industrial disease reform in the 1920s. It was lead by Alice Hamilton, she was a product of Jane Adam’s Hull-House. They helped the women find expert witnesses to testify on their behalf because most scientists were on the company’s side.

Victory!

On June 5, 1928, US Radium agreed to settle with the women's suit. The five dialpainters suit cost U.S. Radium over $ 15,000 apiece. For the first time in American history, an employer was required to pay for its workers’ medical treatment as a result of negligence.
